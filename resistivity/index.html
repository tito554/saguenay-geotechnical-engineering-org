Electrical Resistivity Surveys (VES) in Saguenay

Together, we solve the challenges of tomorrow.

SEE MORE →

The Saguenay graben presents unique subsurface conditions where marine clays overlie fractured Precambrian bedrock. Electrical resistivity surveys following ASTM D6431 provide a non-invasive method to map these transitions before any excavation begins. The NBCC requires site-specific geotechnical data for seismic site classification, and resistivity data supports Vs30 correlation when combined with MASW surveys. Our team runs Schlumberger array VES soundings across Chicoutimi, Jonquière, and La Baie to resolve layering in the thick Laflamme Sea clay deposits that dominate the valley floor.

Resistivity contrasts in Saguenay clay can exceed three orders of magnitude, making VES exceptionally reliable for bedrock mapping.

Scope of work

A recent investigation near the Rivière aux Sables involved a 400-meter VES profile to locate bedrock beneath 25 meters of sensitive clay. The resistivity contrast between the conductive clay (below 10 ohm-m) and the resistive granitic basement (above 2000 ohm-m) was sharp and unambiguous. We placed 72 electrodes with 5-meter spacing, running a dipole-dipole array in parallel with Schlumberger soundings for depth calibration. The resulting 2D inverted section showed a buried bedrock channel not visible in sparse borehole logs. This kind of detail matters in Saguenay, where post-glacial erosion has created irregular rockhead topography that complicates pile design and dewatering planning.

Area-specific notes

Winter surveys in Saguenay present a real challenge. Frozen ground increases contact resistance to the point where standard stainless steel electrodes cannot inject sufficient current. We switch to bentonite-packed electrodes and pre-wet the electrode positions with saline solution when temperatures drop below -15°C. Spring thaw adds another complication: surface meltwater saturates the top meter, creating a low-resistivity layer that can mask deeper targets if not accounted for in the inversion model. Ignoring these seasonal effects leads to misinterpretation of bedrock depth, a costly mistake when designing deep foundations in the Saguenay clay belt.

Quick answers

What depth can a VES survey reach in Saguenay clay?

With a maximum AB/2 electrode separation of 150 meters, investigation depth exceeds 80 meters in the conductive marine clays of the Saguenay graben. Penetration is limited by the current output of the resistivity meter and the low-resistivity near-surface layer, which attenuates the signal. For targets deeper than 100 meters, we recommend combining VES with seismic refraction.

How much does an electrical resistivity survey cost in Saguenay?

A typical VES survey in the Saguenay region ranges from CA$960 to CA$1,600 depending on the number of soundings, electrode array length, and terrain accessibility. 2D imaging profiles are quoted per linear meter. Contact us with your site coordinates for a fixed-price proposal.

Can resistivity detect the sensitive clay layers in Saguenay?

Yes. The Laflamme Sea clays have characteristic resistivity values between 5 and 15 ohm-m due to their elevated pore-water salinity. This signature is distinct from the underlying till (30-80 ohm-m) and granitic bedrock (above 2000 ohm-m). Resistivity alone cannot quantify sensitivity or remolded strength, but it reliably maps layer thickness and continuity.

What surface conditions block resistivity measurements?

Asphalt, concrete slabs, and frozen ground create high contact resistance that prevents current injection. In urban Saguenay areas, we work from unpaved strips or drill small pilot holes through pavement. In winter, we use heated saline solution and bentonite mud at each electrode position to maintain conductivity.
